What is a Literature Review?

‚ùå What it is not:

  • An annotated bibliography.
  • A list of summaries : ‚ÄúAuthor A said X, Author B said Y‚Äù.
  • A comprehensive catalog of everything ever written on a topic.
  • Book reports strung together.

What is a Literature Review?

‚úÖ What it is:

  • Critical analysis of existing research.
  • Synthesis that creates new understanding.
  • An argument about the state of knowledge.
  • A foundation that justifies YOUR research.

Literature Review Scope

Good Research Questions

  • Specific: Not ‚ÄúIs online learning good?‚Äù but ‚ÄúDoes online instruction improve statistical reasoning skills in undergraduate psychology students?‚Äù.
  • Answerable: Can be addressed through research.
  • Significant: Matters to your field.
  • Novel: Addresses a gap or extends existing work.

Questions to Ask Yourself

  1. What EXACTLY am I trying to find out?
  2. What do I NEED to know to answer this?
  3. What are the KEY CONCEPTS in my question?
  4. What DISCIPLINES might have relevant literature?
  5. What‚Äôs my TIME FRAME of interest?

Common Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria

  1. Time Period
    • Last 5 years? 10 years? 20 years?
    • Include seminal older works?
  2. Publication Type
    • Peer-reviewed journals only?
    • Include books, dissertations, conference papers?
    • Grey literature (reports, working papers)?
  3. Geography/Context
    • Specific countries or regions?
    • Global scope?
    • Cultural context matters?
  4. Methodology
    • Quantitative, qualitative, mixed methods?
    • Specific research designs (experiments, surveys, ethnography)?
    • Theoretical vs. empirical?
  5. Language
    • English only?
    • Multiple languages if you can access/read them?
  6. Quality Indicators
    • High-impact journals?
    • Methodological rigor standards?
    • Citation count thresholds?

Pro Tip: Document your criteria!

  • Keep a methods log.
  • You‚Äôll need to justify them later.
  • Helps you stay consistent.
  • Makes your review reproducible.

Strategic Reading


Not all papers deserve equal time.


Strategic reading means knowing WHEN to skim, when to read carefully, and when to study deeply.

Pass 1: SKIM (5-10 minutes per paper)

  • Read: Title, Abstract, Introduction (first few paragraphs), Conclusion
  • Scan: Section headings, figures, tables
  • Decision: Is this relevant to my question? Keep or discard?
  • Outcome: Sort into ‚ÄúHighly Relevant,‚Äù ‚ÄúModerately Relevant,‚Äù ‚ÄúNot Relevant‚Äù

Pass 2: ACTIVE READING (20-30 minutes per paper)

  • Do this for: ‚ÄúHighly Relevant‚Äù papers
  • Read: Full paper but strategically
    • Introduction: What‚Äôs the question and why does it matter?
    • Methods: How did they study it? Appropriate?
    • Results: What did they find? (Read carefully)
    • Discussion: What do THEY think it means?
  • Action: Highlight key points, take structured notes
  • Ask: What‚Äôs the main contribution? How does this relate to MY question?

Pass 3: DEEP READING (1-2 hours per paper)

  • Do this for: 5-10 most critical papers
  • Read: Every word, every citation, every detail
  • Action:
    • Detailed notes on methodology
    • Critique strengths and limitations
    • Follow up on key citations
    • Connect to theoretical frameworks
  • Ask: Can I reproduce their logic? What are unstated assumptions?

Key Principles

  1. Consistency
    • Use the same format for every paper
    • Makes it easier to compare and synthesize later
  2. Distinguish
    • Author‚Äôs words (mark as quotes with page numbers)
    • Your paraphrase (cite but no quotes)
    • Your own analysis/thoughts (clearly marked)
    • This prevents accidental plagiarism
  3. Action-Oriented
    • Always note: ‚ÄúHow does this relate to MY research?‚Äù
    • Tag by themes you‚Äôre seeing emerge
  4. Connected
    • Note relationships to other papers
    • Build your own web of literature

Pro Tips ‚úÖ DO:

  • Take notes immediately after reading (while fresh)
  • Always include page numbers for quotes
  • Tag/categorize by emerging themes
  • Note your gut reactions and questions

Pro Tips ‚ùå DON‚ÄôT:

  • Just highlight in the PDF (you‚Äôll never look again)
  • Rely on memory (you‚Äôll forget)
  • Copy large chunks without paraphrasing (plagiarism risk)
  • Wait until you‚Äôve read 20 papers to start taking notes (overwhelming)

Summary vs. Synthesis



This is where most literature reviews fail.

Summary (What people often do)

Smith (2020) studied online learning and found it was effective for engineering students.

Jones (2021) examined remote education and discovered that students appreciated flexibility.

Kumar (2022) investigated virtual classrooms and showed improved access for rural students.

What‚Äôs wrong?

  • Each paper treated separately
  • No connections between studies
  • No pattern recognition
  • No critical evaluation
  • No argument
  • ü•± Boring to read!

Synthesis (What you should do)

Research consistently demonstrates benefits of online learning, though these vary by context and outcome measure. Effectiveness studies show positive learning outcomes in STEM fields (Smith, 2020; Lee, 2021), while access studies highlight particular benefits for geographically isolated students (Kumar, 2022; Wang, 2023). However, most existing research focuses on undergraduate populations in North American contexts, leaving gaps in our understanding of graduate-level online learning in the Global South (Jones, 2021).

What makes this synthesis?

  • Groups studies by theme (effectiveness vs. access)
  • Identifies patterns (STEM, undergraduate focus)
  • Notes contradictions or variations
  • Identifies gaps
  • Builds toward a ü§© point (justifies new research)

Literature Review Structure

  • Introduction (10-15%) What it does:
    • Sets the context
    • States your research question
    • Explains scope and boundaries
    • Previews organization
    • Hooks the reader
  • Body (70-80%) What it does:
    • Organized by your chosen structure (usually thematic)
    • Each section builds your argument
    • Shows what‚Äôs known, what‚Äôs debated, what‚Äôs missing
    • Connects ideas across studies
  • Conclusion (10-15%) What it does:
    • Synthesizes main findings
    • Highlights key gaps
    • Justifies your research
    • Transitions to your study

Academic Voice

  • Maintain third person (avoid ‚ÄúI think‚Äù)
  • Be authoritative but not absolute
  • Use appropriate hedging: ‚Äúsuggests,‚Äù ‚Äúindicates,‚Äù ‚Äúappears to‚Äù

Top ‚ùå 10 Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Listing studies without synthesis: ‚ÄúAuthor A said X. Author B said Y.‚Äù
  2. Being too broad or too narrow: Balance comprehensiveness with focus
  3. Lack of clear organization: Random order confuses readers
  4. Uncritical acceptance: Evaluate, don‚Äôt just report
  5. Ignoring contradictions: Address conflicting findings
  6. No clear argument/thread: Every section should build toward your gap
  7. Outdated sources: Include recent work (unless historical review)
  8. Missing seminal works: Know the classics in your field
  9. Poor citation practices: Be consistent and accurate
  10. Failing to identify gaps: The whole point is finding what‚Äôs missing!

Summary - 10 Key Points

  1. Literature review = synthesis, not summary
  2. Start with a clear, specific research question
  3. Set inclusion/exclusion criteria systematically
  4. Read strategically using the three-pass method
  5. Take structured notes that support analysis
  6. Ask critical questions, don‚Äôt just accept findings
  7. Identify themes and organize meaningfully
  8. Synthesize across studies - show patterns, contradictions, gaps
  9. Build an argument that justifies YOUR research
  10. Structure clearly and write with purpose
